Part 1: Definitions and Research

Dictionary

Dimension can be a noun or a verb.

Noun:
  • A measure in one direction (e.g., the dimensions of a room).
  • A group of properties necessary and sufficient to determine something (e.g., the surface of a sphere has two dimensions).
  • A coordinate variable assigned to such a property.
  • A lifelike or realistic quality (e.g., dialogue added dimension to her character).
  • A level of existence or consciousness (e.g., a spiritual dimension).
  • An element or factor making up a complete personality or entity (e.g., the social and political dimensions of a problem).
  • Bodily form or proportions (Shakespeare: “My dimensions are as well as compact, my mind as generous, and my shape as true.”)
Verb:
  • To form to the required dimensions (e.g., a table dimensioned to fit the space).

Etymology

  • From Latin dimensio, first appeared in English in the late 14th century.
  • 1520s: bulk, size, extent, capacity.
  • 1920s: component of a situation, realm, or universe.
  • Originally mathematical, later expanded.
  • Plato: higher realm of perfect, unchanging “forms” beyond the physical world.
  • Dimensionalism: reality as a spectrum of interconnected dimensions — physical, psychological, spiritual, mystical.

Thesaurus

Synonyms: aspect, element, extent, height, importance, length, magnitude, proportion, quality, range, scope, size, thickness, vastness.

Antonyms: insignificance, unimportance.

Part 3: Final Dimensions

Based on the overlaps between themes in my experiences, I identified five personal dimensions that define how I understand and design experiences.

  1. Transition
    Change, displacement, new beginnings
    Shaped by experiences of moving, adapting to new environments, and navigating personal growth.
  2. Agency
    Independence, self, choice
    Emerges from moments of persistence, responsibility, and the freedom to influence outcomes.
  3. Flavor
    Sensory, taste, variety
    Draws from cultural identity, food connections, and the sensory richness of new experiences.
  4. Passion
    Energy, drive, ambition
    Informed by creativity, music, personal expression, and the pursuit of meaningful projects.
  5. Nostalgia
    Memory, time, emotional resonance
    Connected to routines, relationships, and moments that continue to influence my identity.

Part 5: Written Reflection

Before approaching this assignment, I thought I already knew which dimensions I would choose. I had concrete ideas for more tangible and physical dimensions that I could design 5 distinct pieces for. However, after taking time and effort to process and diagram this assignment and do ample research on what dimensions mean, I realized that my earlier ideas were too rigid and concrete. This exercise helped me see that interaction design is not limited to the physical or material, but extends to emotional, cognitive, sensual, and memory-based dimensions as well. By focusing on less visible aspects based purely from my own experiences, I was able to understand more how design can shape so many factors of our lives beyond just the surface.
I think using personal experiences as the basis of each of my dimension helped me understand how experiences truly work in the human mind. Everyone has unique experiences and feelings surrounding some event, and emotions play a big part in influencing how we perceive it, highlighting the importance of human-centered design. Exploring each of my chosen dimensions allowed me to see how design can highlight qualities of life that are intangible and unexpected across multiple layers of interaction.
